Death and Controversies

Although Einstein's biological body died in 1955, his head is still active today through a combination of technology and faith. On his deathbed, before dying, Einstein requested that a 3D x-ray scan of his brain was made, in order to "preserve his genius eternally". Unfortunately, the x-ray fried his brain to ashes, as well as his entire head and upper neck. Einstein was extremely healthy and doctors believe he would have probably lived for another 20 years if he hadn't x-rayed his head, or at least if he had done a 2D x-ray instead of a full 3D one. However, many took comfort in the fact that at least Bert the Destroyer destroyed one last thing as he left the physical realm and went into the next realm where there is nothing physical for him to destroy.

Thankfully, modern technology was able to recreate Albert's mind through computer chips, usb sticks, and other electronic things with misleading names. Scientists were able to reproduce Albert's mind by first scanning the ashes of his brain, this time with modern x-ray equipment, instead of the approach Einstein originally took, which was to utilise the oldest, most archaic x-ray machine he could find. After scanning the ashes of Einstein's brain, the x-rayologists threw the ashes into the sea, hoping that the salt water might solidify the ashes back into the shape of a brain. They granted this was a long shot, and were not at all surprised to see that the ashes merely dissolved into the water - however, one of the x-rayologists added that, "maybe if there wasn't so much wind and the brain ash all fell together in a pile into the water, instead of half of it flying back into our faces and clothes, then maybe the salt water plan would have worked..." Having had their ideal plan fail, they went back to their more time-consuming backup idea of using technology to reproduce Einstein's brain. After almost 2 decades of development, the pair of x-rayologists finally realised they needed more than just 2 people, and with relevant expertise, working on the project, because all they had so far was a 3D scan of ashes and a rubbed out blackboard. And just 8 months after that realisation, the project was complete: Einstein's head had successfully been recreated, right down to his desire for manipulation, which is considered to be the source of his genius.

Today, Einstein's recreated head can be found doing science behind a glass display at the Theoretical Museum of Germany. This has caused a lot of controversy. One freelance journalist has argued, "If we are gonna spend taxpayer money on recreating Albert Einstein's head, we should at least be able to touch it," though he also admits, "I don't pay taxes." A security guard at the museum, who shall remain anonymous because they were not wearing a name tag, also pointed out that, "A.I. anywhere is a threat to humanity everywhere," adding, "if we don't destroy that A.I. now, then it's gonna destroy us later, especially if we're talking about Bert the Destroyer, who's got the word 'destroy' in his name". In response to this, the museum director has assured the public that Einstein's head was programmed to not attack others unless it feels this is necessary in order to achieve a scientific discovery.
